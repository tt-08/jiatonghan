## Peer-Reviewed Publications
1. **Risk Preference Elicitation in Finance: Survey vs. Experiment**
    * Fadong Chen, **Jiatong Han**, and Leo Bao (2026). *Journal of Behavioral and Experimental Economics*, 122, 102565.
    * Using data from 108 retail investors on a wealth-management FinTech platform, we elicit risk preferences via both a standard survey and an incentivized Holt–Laury task, and find that the experimental measure outperforms the survey in predicting actual portfolio choices over short and medium horizons.

## Working Papers
1. **Dynamic Peer Influence on Honest Participation and Strategic Misconduct in Performance-based Institutions** *[Job Market Paper]*
    * **Jiatong Han**, Xiaozhi Yang, Yubai Yuan, Fadong Chen, and Colin F. Camerer.
    * Using panel data on 4,868 students and over 764,000 GPS-tracked sessions in a mandatory university running program, we find that peer behavior—among randomly assigned roommates and self-selected dining companions—predicts both subsequent engagement and whether students participate honestly or game the system, with patterns more consistent with social comparison than simple conformism.

2. **Predicting Experimental Asset Bubble Returns using Machine Learning and Emotions**
    * Ross Spoon, Thomas Henning, Mihai Toma, **Jiatong Han**, Cady Ngo, Alec Smith, and Colin Camerer.
    * Combining experimental asset markets with biometric data, we use Support Vector Regression and linear models to show that financial metrics (lagged returns and order book imbalances) outperform biometrics in predicting contemporaneous returns, while individual electrodermal activity predicts personal de-risking and revealed risk aversion; high earners show anticipatory arousal before crashes, whereas low earners display reactionary spikes.

3. **LLM Trading: Analysis of LLM Agent Behavior in Experimental Asset Markets**
    * Thomas Henning, Siddhartha M. Ojha, Ross Spoon, **Jiatong Han**, and Colin F. Camerer.
    * We compare large language models (LLMs) with human traders in a classic experimental-finance market where prices are set endogenously by participants' orders. LLM agents price near fundamental value and form bubbles only weakly, whereas humans consistently generate large bubbles; the gap persists under dividend shocks and experienced runs, underscoring the risk of using LLM-only agents to model human-driven market phenomena.
